The Family Philosophy

Forget flashy. A simple body fake — perfectly timed — will beat any defender. Superior ball control and reading of space will make you look like a different player than kids who've been playing for years. This is where we start.

  • Ball control — the ball does what you tell it
  • First touch — your touch sets you up, never against you
  • Deception — body fakes, weight shifts, selling movement
  • Turns — quick, explosive, ball protected
  • Scanning — know what's behind you before it arrives
  • Anticipation — be in the right place a second early
🎯 Bread & Butter — Master These First
⚽ Ball Control
The ball does exactly what you tell it. No exceptions.
Wall Rebound Control
Pass or strike the ball at a wall and control the rebound. Start with inside of foot, progress to outside foot and sole. Keep ball within one step at all times. Build rhythm and feel — this is deliberate practice.
Solo 5–10 min Needs wall ▶ Demo
Ball Mastery — Inside/Outside/Sole Progression
Touch the ball continuously: inside of foot, outside, sole rolls, toe taps. No pattern — just stay loose and feel the ball. Every surface. Keep it tight — within 12 inches. Do this every single day. 10 minutes of this daily changes your touch in 3 weeks.
Solo 10 min daily ▶ Demo
Tight Space Dribble Circle
Dribble in a very small 5-yard circle — use only tiny touches 1–2 inches from your foot. Keep it close. Change direction every 30 seconds. Head up when possible. This is pure touch development.
Solo 3 × 3 min ▶ Demo
👟 First Touch
Your touch sets you up — never against you.
First Touch Direction Control
Partner passes to you from different angles. Your first touch must move the ball AWAY from where the pass came from — away from pressure, into space. Practice all surfaces. The question you ask on every touch: "where am I going next?" Answer that with your first touch.
Partner 10 min, 40–50 reps ▶ Demo
First Touch Under Pressure
Partner passes quickly at a rapid pace — no time to think. Control in one touch, immediately be ready to move or pass back. This is game-realistic. The pressure forces you to be decisive.
Partner 8 min, 3×15 reps ▶ Demo
Receiving While Scanning
Before receiving, make a deliberate head turn left and right. Then receive the pass and immediately move ball in the direction you just scanned. Links scanning and first touch together — as they are in a real game.
Partner 10 min, 4×12 reps ▶ Demo
🎭 Deception & Feints
Simple. Timed. Committed. That's all deception is.
Basic Body Feint (The One That Actually Works)
Dribble slowly. Touch ball slightly across your body, drop your shoulder hard and shift your weight to sell the fake. Then push ball with outside of foot into open space and accelerate. 90% of defenders will bite. The secret: commitment. Half-hearted fakes don't work. Full commitment does.
Solo first 6 min, 3×10 reps ▶ Demo
Sole Drag Feint
Dribble forward. Drag the sole of your foot back across the ball — this stops your momentum completely and confuses a closing defender. Immediately push ball with outside of foot into space. Simple, game-effective, and most players never see it coming because it's so subtle.
Solo 5 min, 3×15 reps ▶ Demo
Body Feint in Live 1v1
Once the solo version is clean, bring in a passive defender who slowly closes you down. Time your feint to when they commit. As they buy it, explode past. Progress: active defender. This is where it gets real — the timing is everything.
Partner/Defender 8 min, 2×8 reps ▶ Demo
🔄 Turns
Quick, explosive, ball protected. Create space instantly.
Cruyff Turn
Dribble toward a cone at a slight angle. As if you're about to cross or pass, plant your outside foot next to the ball. Use the inside of your other foot to flick the ball sharply behind your plant leg. Explode in the new direction. Used to cut inside from wide positions — extremely effective in real games.
Solo 6 min, 2×12 reps ▶ Demo
Inside Hook Turn at Cone
Dribble to a cone with small, controlled touches. At the cone, use the inside of your foot to hook the ball 180° and immediately accelerate back the way you came. Your body weight shifts explosively. 3 sets × 8 turns each direction.
Solo 7 min, 3×8 reps ▶ Demo
Turn Under Pressure
Partner passes to your feet. Defender applies passive pressure from behind. Receive, execute a turn (your choice), accelerate away. This makes every turn you've drilled game-functional. Builds urgency and timing.
Partner + Defender 8 min, 2×10 reps ▶ Demo
👀 Scanning + Anticipation
Know what's around you before the ball arrives.
Cone Number Awareness Drill
6 numbered cones in a circle around you, 8–10 yards out. Dribble slowly in the center. Every 5–10 touches, partner calls a number. You must look up, find that cone, and dribble toward it immediately. Builds the habit of scanning automatically while in possession.
Partner calling numbers 10 min, 2×15 scans ▶ Demo
Scan Before Every Receive
Simple rule: before every time you receive a pass, you must make a deliberate look left AND right. It must become automatic. Start slow. Partner passes. You scan, receive, move in the open direction. Eventually this becomes muscle memory — you'll do it unconsciously in games.
Partner 10 min, 3×15 reps ▶ Demo
Anticipation — Move Before the Pass
In a 4v2 or small-sided game, challenge yourself: can you start moving to receive BEFORE your teammate has decided to pass to you? Read their body, their weight, where the defender is. A player who arrives half a second early looks telepathic — it's just anticipation trained over time.

Writing & Research

This is your long-term intellectual identity. Research is how you contribute something original to the world — not just consume information, but generate it. Every great researcher started by learning the craft before they had anything to say. Start building that foundation now.

🔬 Academic Research
Understanding what research is and how to enter the field.
What Is Academic Research?
Academic research is the systematic process of investigating a question no one has definitively answered. It involves a literature review (what's already known), a hypothesis or question, a methodology, data or evidence, analysis, and a conclusion. The goal is to add something new — even a small piece — to human knowledge.
Foundation
Fields to Explore
Behavioral science, sports science, psychology, economics, public health, computer science. You don't have to pick one yet — read across fields and notice where your questions live. Research careers often start with a single genuine curiosity.
Exploration
Start a Research Journal
Keep a running document of questions that genuinely interest you. Not answers — questions. "Why do athletes perform better under pressure sometimes?" "What causes habit formation?" These questions become your first research directions.
SoloDaily habit
🧭 How to Do Research
The actual process — step by step.
Step 1 — Find a Question
Start with genuine curiosity. A good research question is specific, answerable, and not already fully solved. Bad: "What is motivation?" Good: "Does goal-setting improve sprint performance in adolescent athletes?"
Process
Step 2 — Literature Review
Before doing any original work, read everything already published on your topic. Use Google Scholar, PubMed, or JSTOR. Summarize what's known, what's debated, and what's missing. That gap is where your research lives.
Process
Step 3 — Methodology
How will you answer your question? Survey? Experiment? Data analysis? Case study? Each method has strengths and weaknesses. Choose the one that fits your question and resources. Start simple — complexity comes later.
Process
Step 4 — Analyze & Write
Collect your data or evidence, analyze it honestly (including results that don't support your hypothesis), and write it up clearly. Research writing is precise and structured: Abstract → Introduction → Methods → Results → Discussion → Conclusion.
Process
📖 Read the Work of Others
You can't write what you haven't read. Read widely and deeply.
How to Read a Research Paper
Don't read start to finish. Use the IMRaD method: read the Abstract first (is this relevant?), then the Introduction (what's the question?), then jump to the Discussion/Conclusion (what did they find?), then go back to Methods/Results if you need detail. Most papers can be assessed in 10–15 minutes this way.
Skill
Where to Find Papers
Google Scholar (scholar.google.com) — free abstracts, often free PDFs. PubMed — biomedical research. JSTOR — humanities and social science. Semantic Scholar — AI-powered research discovery. ResearchGate — authors often post free copies of their work.
Resource
Follow Researchers You Respect
Find researchers in your area of interest and follow their work. Google Scholar lets you follow authors and get alerts when they publish. Twitter/X is where a lot of researchers share new findings informally. This keeps you current.
Habit
📝 Getting Published
The path from research to publication.
Start with High School Journals
You don't need to publish in Nature. Start with journals designed for high school researchers: Journal of Emerging Investigators (JEI), Regeneron Science Talent Search, Concord Review (humanities), and the American Junior Academy of Science. These are legitimate, peer-reviewed, and taken seriously.
Entry point
The Submission Process
Write your paper → format it to the journal's guidelines → submit → peer review (reviewers critique your work anonymously) → revise → acceptance or rejection → publication. Rejection is normal and part of the process. Every researcher gets rejected.
Process
Co-Author with a Mentor
The fastest path to publication as a student is finding a professor or graduate student willing to mentor you. You contribute the work, they provide guidance and credibility. Reach out cold via email — one in ten will respond positively. That's enough.
Strategy

🛠️ Tools
The researcher's toolkit — start using these now.
Zotero — Reference Manager
Free tool to collect, organize, cite, and share research sources. Install the browser extension and every paper you find gets saved automatically. Generates citations in any format (APA, MLA, Chicago). Essential for any serious research project.
Free → zotero.org
Google Scholar
Search engine for academic papers. Set up alerts for keywords in your research area — you'll get emailed when new papers are published. Create a profile to track your own citations one day.
Free → scholar.google.com
Notion or Obsidian — Research Notes
Build a personal knowledge base. Every paper you read, every idea you have, every question that comes up — log it. Obsidian links ideas together like a web. Notion is more structured. Either one creates a compounding system: the more you add, the more valuable it becomes.
Free → notion.so
Connected Papers
Enter any research paper and get a visual graph of all related papers — what it cites and what cites it. Incredible for mapping a new field quickly and finding the key foundational papers you need to read.
Free → connectedpapers.com
Overleaf — LaTeX Writing
The standard tool for writing academic papers in science and math. LaTeX produces professional-quality documents with proper formatting for equations, citations, and figures. Free online version at overleaf.com. Learning this now puts you ahead of most undergraduates.
Free tier → overleaf.com
